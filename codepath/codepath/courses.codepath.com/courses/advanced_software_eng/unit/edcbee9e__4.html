Week 4 - Binary Trees

This week we will be covering the fundamentals of binary trees and common ways they appear in interviews.

Goals

  • Know how to search for values in binary search tree in optimal way
  • Understand and be able to implement the following types of traversals, including when to use them and their tradeoffs
    • In-order, pre-order, post-order
    • BFS
    • DFS
  • Understand how to implement at least one traversal in iterative approach
  • Understand the concept of augmented trees and when they can be useful

Pseudocoding

In this interactive session we will practice our pseudocoding skills. Pseudocode is a great way to map out your ideas and plans before starting to write actual code.

Once you have a general idea of how to approach a problem it could be tempting to jump right into coding. However, without having an complete idea of how you're going to solve the problem, you could end up going down a series of badly executed steps.

Pseudocoding allows you to map out your thought process before getting into the details of code syntax. It also allows the interviewer to visualize your thought process more clearly so iterations and improvements can be made if necessary.

While writing pseudocode, it's usually a good idea to think about the following:

  • If it's a recursive solution, what will be your base case?

  • Are there helper methods that you can stub out to simplify the problem and code?

  • What will your for loop or while loop look like?

  • Extra reading on improving your pseudocode skills

Questions

For the following questions:

  1. Understand the problem. Besides the given example(s) try to come up with some of your own examples for the problem.
  2. Match. Does this problem match any other patterns you've seen before?
  3. Prototype and pseudocode. Using one of the examples you came up with in Step 1 walk through a high level implementation. Make sure you're not missing critical edge cases.

Example of a working through a problem with pseudocode

Keep in mind that during interviews coding questions are usually allotted 30-40 minutes. Given that time constraint, you should aim to spend around 15-20 minutes on each problem:

  • Level Order Traversal Bottom-Up
  • Min Depth of Binary Tree
  • BST to Linked List
  • Subtree of Another Tree
  • Find Duplicate Subtrees
  • Most Frequent Subtree Sum

Mock Interview

This segment of the class will be a practice interviewing session where everyone will be paired up with a partner. This guide gives an insight on how to:

  1. Conduct a productive mock interview (when playing the role of the interviewer)
  2. Be a proactive interviewee

You should spend 30 minutes on one problem before switching roles with your partner and moving on to their question.

First, introduce yourself to your partner. Then, one person should start off as the interviewer, and share the collabedit link they created before the session, where the two of you will collaborate.

Challenge Questions:

  1. Create balanced BST from sorted array

  2. Vertical order (column) traversal for BST

If you and your partner both finish your questions within the allotted time, try tackling Find Largest Value in Each Tree Row together

Role of the interviewer:

  • Help the interviewee understand the problem and answer any clarifying questions
  • Provide useful hints to help move the problem along
  • Help the interviewee debug their code once they’ve mapped out a solution

What the interviewer should NOT do:

  • Not pay attention while the interviewee is working through their solution
  • Blatantly tell the interviewee the most optimal solution within 10 minutes of them being stuck. Instead, try to provide helpful hints that will guide them in the right direction
  • Take over typing for the interviewee because they are stuck

Role of the interviewee:

  • Understand the question being asked and think through the trade-offs of different approaches
  • Work through the problem and come to a working solution

What the interviewee should NOT do:

  • Immediately start writing code after reading the question. Instead, ask clarifying questions and discuss the approach you have in mind. Talk out loud about possible approaches and their tradeoffs. Think through a couple test cases to see if your approach would work.
  • Stay silent for more than 3 minutes. If you find yourself having a mental block, try to articulate what part you’re stuck on. Perhaps there’s certain terminology you’re not confident with or you can’t visualize a part of the problem you’re trying to solve. Talking out loud helps your interviewer guide you.
